Who this is for

This guide is for people in participatory churches who regularly find themselves in conversations where somebody tells them their marriage is hard. Small group leaders. Prayer partners. The person who always gets pulled aside after service. Ministry team members. Home church hosts. Anyone whose church has a flat enough structure that pastoral care happens in living rooms and coffee shops, not just in pastors' offices.

Most of what people bring to you is ordinary marriage conflict — things better communication and some grace can work through. But a meaningful minority is not ordinary marriage conflict. It is psychological abuse and coercive control, and responding to it the way you would respond to ordinary conflict makes it worse. This guide is about how to tell the difference, what to do, and what not to do. It is short on purpose.

The core message

If you get one thing from this document

Couples counseling with an active abuser makes the victim less safe, not more. Don't do it. Don't pray with them together about the marriage. Don't mediate. Don't facilitate reconciliation conversations. Meet with each of them separately, or meet only with the one who reached out. This is the single most important thing lay helpers get wrong, and it costs victims years.

Why lay helpers need this specifically

Pastoral-care training, when it exists, mostly sits with ordained staff. But in churches with participatory models, the people actually receiving disclosures of marriage trouble are often volunteers: the small group leader, the worship team friend, the older couple in the home church. The pattern is well-documented. Research on domestic violence in church contexts consistently finds that victims disclose to trusted lay people more often than to pastors — and that those lay people, with the best intentions, often respond in ways that extend the abuse rather than interrupt it.

The specific failure modes are narrow and predictable. They are covered below. This guide exists so that the next time somebody in your group starts to tell you something that sounds wrong, you have a better script than whatever instinct is going to produce in the moment.

What psychological abuse and coercive control actually are

Domestic violence is not just hitting. The legal definition in Colorado and the clinical definition in the research both recognize a pattern of behavior designed to control another person — through intimidation, isolation, financial control, surveillance, threats, humiliation, and the systematic degradation of the other person's autonomy and sense of reality. Physical violence may be part of it. It doesn't have to be.

The technical term is coercive control. The older term is psychological abuse. They describe the same thing. In the research, psychological abuse without physical violence produces mental-health harm equivalent to or greater than physical violence. This is counterintuitive and important: the absence of bruises does not mean the situation is mild. Some of the most dangerous abusers never hit.

Being the first line of defense is itself a duty

If you are reading this document, the most likely reason is that someone has come to you with their marriage. Not to a professional counselor, not to a pastor, not to a domestic violence advocate. To you. Maybe in a small group. Maybe in your kitchen. Maybe over coffee after church. You are the first line of defense. What you do in the next conversation, and the one after that, and the one after that, will shape whether this person ever reaches a professional — or doesn't.

This is not metaphor. The research is unambiguous on it. Survivors of domestic violence disclose to friends and family more often than to any other category of helper, including professionals. Most people in abusive relationships will never speak to a domestic violence advocate. They will tell one or two trusted people, watch how those people respond, and decide based on that response whether disclosure is safe at all. The first responder you are matters more than any later professional, because the first responder is the gatekeeper to whether a later professional ever gets called.

"The justice system will handle it"

If your instinct, when handed material like this, is to say something like "well, the legal system exists for a reason" or "the courts are the safeguard" or "if it's really that bad, the police will get involved" — please understand that this is empirically wrong, in ways that have been measured and published, and that acting on that instinct is one of the documented mechanisms by which abuse continues.

The statistics are these. Roughly half of all domestic violence is never reported to police at all (Bureau of Justice Statistics, NCVS 2006–2015 and follow-up reports). For coercive control specifically — abuse without consistent physical violence — reporting rates are dramatically lower; some studies place reporting rates for non-physical IPV under 1% (cited in Domestic Violence in the United States meta-review). Of cases that are reported, only about 39% result in arrest. Of arrests, only about 27% result in prosecution. Of prosecutions, only about 14% result in conviction (Garner & Maxwell, 2009; Reaves, 2017). That means roughly 1 in 14 reported domestic violence incidents — and a far smaller fraction of all incidents — ever produces a criminal conviction.

That is the system you are deferring to when you say "the courts will handle it." That system catches a small minority of abusers, on a slow timeline, after substantial additional harm has typically already occurred. It is not designed to find abuse on its own. It is designed to respond to abuse that has already been disclosed, reported, documented, and witnessed by trained people. Almost every step of that pipeline starts with someone — a friend, a family member, a pastor, a small group leader, a church friend — believing the survivor and helping the disclosure happen.

If you are not that person — if when a disclosure happens, you become an obstacle instead of a bridge to someone trained — you are not "letting the system handle it." You are part of why the system never gets the case.

Why the obligation has moral and not just practical weight

The framing of this guide so far has been practical: here are the things that help, here are the things that hurt, here is the literature. There is also a moral dimension that should not be left implicit.

  • Children are watching. Witnessing coercive control produces measurable harm to children equivalent to direct physical abuse — the research is consistent on this point (Callaghan et al., 2018; Katz, 2016, 2022). If a parent in your community is being coercively controlled, the children are being harmed in real time, every day, whether or not they are themselves the targets. Refusing to engage with this — declining to read the materials, declining to learn the warning signs, declining to act on what you do see — is not a neutral choice. It is a choice with real-time consequences for children whose parent is currently being harmed.
  • Disclosure is a one-shot event. A survivor often discloses to one person. If that person responds badly — by deflecting, scolding, recommending couples counseling, suggesting forgiveness, or invoking the legal system as a reason not to act — the survivor commonly does not try again for years, sometimes ever. The harm from a botched first response is not just the missed help in that conversation. It is the closing of a door the survivor may never open again.
  • You don't get to opt out by being unqualified. "I'm not a professional, so I shouldn't be involved" is not the right conclusion from limited training. The right conclusion is "I'm not a professional, so my job is the handoff to one." A pediatrician who notices possible signs of child abuse does not say "I'm not a forensic interviewer, so I'll do nothing." They make the report and let the trained professionals take it from there. The same logic applies to lay helpers in churches.
  • The category exists for a reason. Mandatory reporters exist as a category in the law because society has recognized that some kinds of harm are sufficiently grave, sufficiently hidden, and sufficiently dependent on observers' willingness to act that we cannot rely on victims alone to surface them. Coercive control is not always within the formal mandatory-reporting framework, but the moral logic is identical. If you are positioned to see it and you do nothing, your inaction is part of the harm.
If someone hands you this document or one like it

Read it. Sit with it. Notice if the impulse arises to dismiss it, scold the person who handed it to you, or wave it off with "the legal system will handle it." That impulse is documented in the research as one of the most reliable mechanisms by which abuse persists in tight-knit communities — religious and secular alike. It is not curiosity. It is not wisdom. It is the predictable response of someone who is being asked to do something hard and doesn't want to.

The person who handed you this material is not insulting you. They are extending you the trust of believing you can hear it. If your instinct is to make them the problem instead of engaging with the content, that instinct is the warning sign, not them.

What this looks like in practice

Concretely, being a responsible first line of defense means:

  • Doing the reading before you need it, not after. The free curriculum at churchcares.com takes about 6 hours and is the single best preparation available. If your church has small group leaders, prayer team members, or an elder doing marriage counseling, those people should be through that curriculum before they hear their first disclosure, not after their first mishandled one.
  • Treating disclosures as the high-stakes moments they are. Not as conversations to manage. Not as occasions to display pastoral wisdom. Not as opportunities to recommend a book or a sermon series. Moments where the survivor's safety, the children's safety, and the future trajectory of a family are all in play, and where what you do in the next ten minutes meaningfully matters.
  • Knowing your handoffs cold. Hotline number. Local advocate. DV-informed therapist. Have these in your phone. Know how to pass them along without making the survivor feel rejected.
  • Holding yourself accountable to these standards when you fall short. If you have responded to a disclosure in one of the ways this document warns against — asked the victim what their role in the problem was, recommended couples counseling, deflected to "the legal system," scolded the person who tried to inform you — the right response is not to defend your past response. It is to learn, change course, and reach back out to the people you may have failed. The cost of admitting a mistake is much smaller than the cost of doubling down on it.

None of this requires becoming an expert. It requires taking seriously that you are already the front line, whether you wanted to be or not, and acting accordingly.

Warning signs, in three registers

What you might see in the room

When you're with a couple together, or when one of them is describing the other:

  • One partner speaks for both. Answers questions directed at the other. Corrects their account. "What she means is…"
  • One partner monitors the other's face. Checks their reaction before answering. Apologizes and revises when the other shifts posture.
  • Asymmetric presentation. One is warm, articulate, self-deprecating, charming. The other is shut down, vague, flat, or over-apologetic. The difference is larger than personality would explain.
  • "We both need to work on things" from one partner while the other absorbs all blame. Listen for who claims fault and who accepts it.
  • Pathologizing the partner. "She's having a mental health crisis." "He's on the spectrum, he doesn't mean things the way they sound." "She's being influenced by people outside the marriage." The partner is being reframed as defective or compromised rather than disagreeing.
  • Stories that don't add up. Events the calmer partner describes don't match things you've observed, or shift each time they're retold.

What to ask about, if you're meeting with one person alone

Not as an interrogation. As part of ordinary caring conversation, when relevant:

  • Money. Does your partner control the money? Do you have to ask permission or account for spending? Do you have your own bank account?
  • Isolation. Have you stopped seeing friends or family over the last few years? Is that a choice that feels like yours?
  • Surveillance. Does your partner check your phone, messages, location, or social media?
  • Fear. Do you find yourself being careful about what you say or do to avoid their reaction? Do you feel afraid of them, even sometimes?
  • Threats. Has your partner ever threatened to take the kids, leave you destitute, harm themselves, or expose something about you?
  • Children and pets. Has your partner hurt or threatened your children or pets? Do they use the kids against you?
  • The eggshells test. Do you manage your behavior to avoid their anger? Is there a long list of things you don't do because of how they'd react?

A single "yes" to any of these is worth paying attention to. Multiple "yes" answers, or a "yes" to fear, is a clear indicator of coercive control.

Red flags that lift this out of "marriage conflict" entirely

  • Any history of physical violence, including "just once." Grabbing, blocking a door, throwing things, restraining, shoving — all count, even if nobody was injured.
  • Any sexual coercion. Marriage does not make consent automatic. Pressured sex is sexual abuse.
  • Threats to kill, including "I'd rather be dead than lose you," "I'd rather we were both dead," or "nobody else will ever have you." These are not romantic. They are statistically significant lethality indicators in the research.
  • Weapons in the home, especially when combined with any of the above.
  • Escalation in frequency or severity of incidents over recent weeks or months.
  • Strangulation or choking, ever, even once. Non-fatal strangulation is one of the strongest predictors of future homicide in the research. Take it seriously and get professional help involved immediately.
  • Framing the partner as spiritually compromised, possessed, or needing to be "fixed" or "cast out." When a partner starts describing the other in cosmological terms — demonic influence, spiritual darkness, needing deliverance — this is a serious warning sign independent of any threat of violence. The research calls it ideational reclassification. The language of necessary correction often precedes behavior the person would not otherwise permit themselves.
If you see the last one specifically

Don't wait. That person should be talking to a trained domestic violence advocate in the near term, not the distant future.

Seven things well-meaning helpers do that make it worse

These are not theoretical. They are the things lay helpers and undertrained pastors do every day in churches, with love in their hearts, that cause measurable harm. Survivors report them consistently in qualitative research.

1. Suggesting couples counseling

Couples counseling with an active abuser is contraindicated. Every major DV organization and every evidence-based training curriculum agrees on this. It assumes both partners can speak freely and share responsibility, neither of which is true in coercive control. Anything the victim discloses in session becomes material the abuser will use later. The abuser learns the victim's language and uses it to manipulate more effectively. Counseling with the abuser present is not a neutral failure; it actively arms them.

What to do instead: Separate individual support. The victim needs a DV-informed counselor or advocate. The abuser needs a batterer intervention program — not anger management, not marriage counseling, not a men's ministry. Batterer intervention programs are specifically designed for this and they are not the same thing as other programs that sound similar.

2. Pushing reconciliation or forgiveness prematurely

Victims are often told to forgive, to restore the marriage, to "move forward." Forgiveness in the biblical sense is a real and good thing; rushing it in the context of ongoing abuse is not forgiveness, it's pressure on the victim to absorb more harm. Reconciliation requires sustained, demonstrated change in the abuser — not an apology, not tears, not a promise, not a season of better behavior. Years of change verified by professionals. Anything less puts the victim back in danger.

What to do instead: Forgiveness is between the person and God, on their timeline. Reconciliation is conditional on change that hasn't happened yet. You do not need to push either one. The goal right now is safety.

3. Misapplying submission or headship

Telling a wife she needs to submit more, be gentler, pray harder, or that "God hates divorce" gives theological cover to abuse. It is exactly what the abuser wants said. Even in churches that hold to headship theology, there is no reading of Ephesians 5 that asks wives to submit to sin against them. The Southern Baptist Convention — not a theologically progressive body — explicitly resolved that "biblical headship blesses, honors, and protects wives and children and does not require them to submit to sin or to abuse." If that denomination can say it, you can say it.

What to do instead: Be explicit. "What is happening to you is not God's plan for your marriage. You are not failing as a wife / husband. Your safety comes first." You don't need to win a theological argument. You need to remove the theological weight the abuser is putting on the victim's shoulders.

4. Blaming the victim, even gently

"What part did you play in this?" "Have you examined your own heart?" "Sometimes we provoke responses we don't intend." All of this, however kindly meant, shifts moral labor onto the person being harmed. Abuse is not a communication problem the victim contributed to. It is a choice the abuser made. Imperfection in the victim does not cause or justify the pattern.

What to do instead: "This is not your fault. What is being done to you is wrong, period." Say it out loud. Victims typically minimize, not exaggerate; they have been told for years that the problem is them. Your job is to not be one more voice confirming that message.

5. Breaking confidentiality to the abuser

Telling the abuser — or anyone who might tell the abuser — what the victim disclosed is dangerous. Not awkward, not unwise, dangerous. The victim's disclosure is a moment of trust that has material safety consequences. An abuser finding out their partner "told on them" to a church friend is a documented trigger for escalation. This includes telling the abuser's family, telling mutual friends, telling the small group, telling the pastor if the victim asked you not to, and telling your own spouse if there's any risk of it getting back.

What to do instead: What they share, stays. The only exceptions are: (a) mandatory reporting if children are being abused (know your state's law), (b) if they give you explicit permission to loop someone in, or (c) if they are in imminent danger and you need to call 911. That's it.

6. Believing the abuser's repentance at face value

Abusers are often charming, articulate, and remorseful-seeming. They can cry. They can quote Scripture. They can appear genuinely broken. Research on perpetrator desistance — actual sustained change — is clear that it looks nothing like the week-long repentance cycle most abusers produce on demand. Real change involves identity-level shifts, specific (not diffuse) guilt, engagement with violence-focused intervention over years, sustained behavior change when nobody is watching, and willingness to restructure the relationships that enabled the pattern. An emotional apology in your living room is not evidence of any of those things.

What to do instead: Believe performance is performance until there is sustained, verifiable evidence otherwise. Ask: has this person engaged with a batterer intervention program, and for how long? Is the victim reporting sustained behavior change, not just a calm period? If you can't answer those questions with years of evidence, the repentance is not yet what it looks like.

7. Trying to handle it yourself

You are not trained for this, and the stakes are too high to learn on the job. Pastors with seminary training and years of experience routinely get this wrong; lay helpers in small groups and home churches have even less to work with. The people in your church who are competent to help long-term are almost certainly not in your church.

What to do instead: Your job is a hand-off, not a solution. Listen. Believe. Keep confidentiality. Help the person connect to a domestic violence professional — a local advocate, the National Hotline, a DV-informed therapist, a legal-aid organization. Stay present as ongoing support. Let the actual work happen with people who know how to do it.

What to do when someone discloses

When it's happening in front of you — in a small group, on a walk, in the kitchen after dinner — keep these in this order:

  • Believe them. Say it out loud. "I hear you. I believe you." Victims typically minimize. If what they're describing sounds concerning, it is probably worse than the version they are giving you.
  • Say it's not their fault. Explicitly. "This is not your fault. What is being done to you is wrong."
  • Don't ask "why don't you just leave." Leaving is the most dangerous moment statistically; asking that question is both painful and shows you don't know the landscape. Instead: "What do you need right now?"
  • Ask about immediate safety. "Are you safe tonight? Are your children safe? Is there somewhere you can go if you need to?"
  • Don't confront the abuser. Ever. It puts the victim in danger. This feels satisfying and noble. Don't do it.
  • Offer the handoff. "There are people who do this for a living. Would it help if I gave you the number for the domestic violence hotline?" Offer; don't push.
  • Keep what they shared confidential. Even from your spouse if the situation is complicated. Even from the pastor if they asked you not to.
  • Stay in their life. Don't disappear after the disclosure. Don't make the conversation the whole relationship, either. Be a friend who also happens to know.
  • Document what was shared, privately. Date, approximate content, in a place the abuser can't access. Not to use against anyone — to corroborate the timeline later if it becomes relevant.

When to get professional help involved — urgently

Call or strongly encourage them to call, today or this week, if any of these are present:

  • Threats to kill — the victim, the abuser, the kids, anyone.
  • Non-fatal strangulation / choking, ever.
  • Access to firearms combined with escalation.
  • Sudden escalation in frequency or severity in recent weeks.
  • The abuser has begun framing the victim in moral, spiritual, or pathologizing terms ("she's mentally unwell," "she needs deliverance").
  • The victim says they are afraid of what the abuser might do.
  • Sexual coercion or assault.
  • Pregnancy in the relationship — pregnancy is a documented elevated-risk period.
  • Recent separation or stated intent to leave — the weeks around separation are statistically the most dangerous.

"Urgently" does not mean dramatically. It means: the person should be talking to a trained DV advocate soon, not someday. The advocate can do risk assessment, safety planning, and connect them to resources you can't.

A few things worth knowing about specific situations

When the victim is neurodivergent

Autistic and ADHD adults are at substantially elevated risk of intimate partner abuse, for reasons that include lifelong masking, a history of being told their perceptions are wrong, smaller social networks that make isolation easier, and the abuser's ability to pathologize neurodivergent traits as proof the victim is "difficult" or "unwell." Distress in neurodivergent victims may not look like what you expect — it may present as flat affect, over-accommodation, apparent compliance, or shutdown rather than visible fear. Do not read "calm" as "fine."

A partner who frames their spouse as "mentally ill" or "having meltdowns" or "on the spectrum, she doesn't understand" may be describing a real diagnosis; they may also be using that framing as a tool of control. Both things can be true at once, and the second does not excuse the first.

When the family involves immigrants or first-generation community members

In many immigrant community contexts — including significant portions of the Latino and African-origin communities in Northern Colorado — the abuser's family of origin may be directly involved in the pattern. Colorado law and international DV research both recognize "in-law abuse" as a distinct category of domestic violence. A mother-in-law who screams at the wife, a father-in-law who instructs his son to discipline his wife, extended family who discredit the victim to the rest of the community — these are not "cultural differences" that should be tolerated or worked around. They are infrastructure of abuse in the coercive-control framework.

Additionally, language barriers, immigration status concerns (including fear that leaving will affect a visa or residency), and community pressure to protect the family's reputation all compound the victim's entrapment. Advocates at the organizations above can work in Spanish and through interpreter services, and DV shelter services are available regardless of immigration status.

If the person disclosing is worried about their immigration status, the Rocky Mountain Immigrant Advocacy Network (RMIAN) and Colorado Legal Services can provide specific guidance. Never, under any circumstances, threaten or imply that leaving could result in deportation — that is itself an abuse tactic and has happened in church contexts with devastating results.

When children are involved

In Colorado, clergy and many church workers are mandatory reporters of child abuse. Witnessing domestic violence is itself recognized as a form of harm to children in the research — the mental-health impact on children who witness psychological abuse matches that of children who are physically abused themselves. The phrase "but the kids love their dad" does not mean the kids are unaffected.

If children are being directly harmed, or if their safety is at imminent risk, the mandatory reporting obligation applies. Know your state's law. If you are a volunteer and unsure whether you are a mandatory reporter, err on the side of calling — you can consult the DV hotline or a DV advocate anonymously to think it through before making any formal report.

When the abuser is a leader or respected member of your church

This is the case where church communities most reliably fail victims. The abuser is often deeply embedded, well-liked, serving in ministry, generous with their time, praying publicly in gifted ways. The victim discloses and the community cannot reconcile the disclosure with the person they know — so they reflexively protect the person they know. This is exactly the mechanism that lets abuse persist in church communities for decades.

The research is clear: abusers in churches are disproportionately in visible service and leadership. Their public persona is not evidence of their private behavior. Believing the victim when the accused is someone you respect is harder, and it is the thing the situation requires. If the accused is in a counseling, mentoring, or leadership role where they have access to others, that needs to be paused — quietly, pending actual evaluation, not as punishment — because others are at risk.

What not to do, in one place

The do-not list
  • Don't do couples counseling. Don't mediate. Don't facilitate reconciliation.
  • Don't confront the abuser.
  • Don't tell anyone who might tell the abuser.
  • Don't push forgiveness or reconciliation on the victim's timeline.
  • Don't quote "God hates divorce."
  • Don't ask the victim what their role in the problem is.
  • Don't ask "why don't you just leave."
  • Don't take the abuser's tears as evidence of change.
  • Don't try to solve it yourself.
  • Don't disappear after the disclosure.
  • Don't make the abuse the entire relationship.

If your group wants to go further

Free, comprehensive, and widely endorsed: churchcares.com offers a free 12-lesson video curriculum, "Becoming a Church That Cares Well for the Abused," developed by evangelical trauma counselors, attorneys, DV advocates, and batterer-intervention specialists. It is the single best starting point for a small-group leader team or elder advisory council to work through together over a few weeks. Handbook and videos are free.

One book, if you read only one: Why Does He Do That? by Lundy Bancroft. Thirty years of working with abusive men in intervention programs; explains the mindset and tactics in language that's accessible without being dumbed down. Not written from a Christian framework but compatible with one.

One book, if you want the Christian register: The Emotionally Destructive Marriage by Leslie Vernick. Written by a Christian licensed counselor; distinguishes "difficult" marriages from "destructive" ones in a way that lay counselors find useful.

For training specifically on coercive control recognition: The MEND Project (themendproject.com) runs responder training specifically on the patterns this document describes. Called to Peace Ministries (calledtopeace.org) provides pastor and lay-leader training from a Christian perspective.

One last thing

You are not going to handle every case perfectly. You will get things wrong. The point is not to become an expert — you won't, and trying to will hurt people. The point is to know enough to not do the specific things that extend abuse, to believe people when they tell you what's happening, to keep what they share safe, and to hand them off to people who can actually help. Those four things, consistently, make lay helpers in participatory churches a meaningful protective factor in the lives of abuse survivors rather than one more voice telling them the problem is them.

Most people in your church are not in abusive relationships. A smaller number are. The ones who are will tell someone eventually, if they tell anyone. That someone is often you. Knowing what to do in that conversation is worth the hour it took to read this.
